The Real Reason You Can't Keep Studying Japanese

And what you need to change starting today

Chapter 01

Why You Can't Stay Consistent

Many Japanese learners struggle with the same frustrations: "I can't improve because I'm not confident enough" or "I just can't keep studying." Before anything else, I want you to stop and ask yourself — why can't you stay consistent? What is the real reason?

In most cases, the problem lies in a misalignment in your mindset, your approach, and the system you're using to learn.

It's Not About Whether the Information Is "Right" — It's About Whether It Fits You

Many people, including myself, share advice and methods about learning Japanese. Each of us shares based on our own experiences and perspectives, so naturally, the content and style vary widely.

What matters is not whether that information is universally "correct," but whether it works for you. Even if a method is scientifically proven to be effective, that doesn't mean it will work for every single learner. On the other hand, a method that is generally considered inefficient may turn out to be the best approach for you — if it fits the way you learn.

The Importance of Asking "Why Is This Good?"

Recommending a study method or copying one is not that hard. But being able to clearly explain why a method works is surprisingly difficult.

When you take advice like "this textbook is great" or "this method is highly recommended" at face value and try it yourself — only to find it doesn't work — that experience starts to stack up. Eventually, you begin to think: "Maybe Japanese just isn't for me." That kind of thinking kills your confidence and drains your motivation.

When things aren't going well, having the ability to ask "why?" gives you the power to figure out your next step.

"Does It Fit Me?" Is Determined by Understanding, Not Preference

When judging whether a learning method fits you, it's risky to rely purely on gut feeling. What truly matters is whether you understand what that method is training, and what purpose it serves.

Take passive listening as an example. In the podcast I create and share publicly, I ask listeners to do one thing first: listen with your ears only, without reading the transcript. There's a specific reason I ask for this — and it comes down to what your brain is actually doing when text is in front of you versus when it isn't.

That reason connects directly to something most learners never think about: the difference between what you're training and what you actually need to build.

Before choosing any study method, ask yourself: "Do I actually understand what this is training — and does it match what I'm trying to build?"

Most learners have never asked this question. And that gap is exactly where Turn Japanese into a Usable Skill begins.

Being "Bad at Studying" Is Not a Lack of Ability

In my view, self-study is something anyone can do. There are differences in how naturally it comes to each person. But "being bad at studying" does not mean you lack the ability to learn.

In most cases, it simply means your mindset or way of thinking about learning is slightly off. Before focusing on methods, it may be more important to re-examine how you approach learning itself.

Chapter 02

Before You Decide That Self-Study Doesn't Work for You

Anyone has the ability to learn Japanese on their own. That is not in question. So why do so many people feel that self-study just doesn't work for them?

Your Environment Is Not an Excuse

It is true that learning environments differ greatly from person to person. Someone who lives in Japan and is surrounded by Japanese every day has a different experience from someone who lives in a place where Japanese is never spoken. That difference is undeniable.

But that doesn't mean you should give up because you don't live in Japan. Economic circumstances also vary. Some people can buy as many textbooks as they want. Others can only study from a single secondhand book.

The feeling of "why can't I do what other people can?" is completely understandable. But staying stuck in that mindset won't change your reality. Instead of comparing yourself to others, focus on what you want to do, how you want to get there, and what your goal really is.

Have You Really Tried Everything?

There are many people around the world who have no choice but to learn on their own. For those people, doing it yourself is the only path forward.

If you're going to say "I can't do self-study," say it only after you've truly tried every option available to you.

But most people haven't — not because they lack willpower, but because they don't yet have a system that fits their real life and points them in the right direction. That's what this book is designed to give you.

Chapter 03

Rethinking Japanese Learning With a Wider View

When most people decide to learn Japanese, they start by opening a vocabulary book and working through grammar from the beginning. There's nothing wrong with that. But what you consider "obvious" or "normal" may not actually be so obvious at all.

What Japanese People's Experience With English Can Teach Us

In Japan, English education is compulsory. Students study English for six years across middle and high school. The vast majority of Japanese people have studied English for years.

Even people who excelled in English class and scored highly on exams often find themselves speechless when a foreign tourist asks them for directions.

Why does this happen? The reason is clear. Japan's English education system has focused on evaluating grammatical accuracy and knowledge — not on developing the actual ability to speak. Students can understand grammar, know vocabulary, follow along with listening — but when it comes to expressing themselves out loud in real time, the words don't come.

This is not just a Japanese problem. If your Japanese learning follows the same pattern — knowledge first, usage indefinitely postponed — the same outcome waits at the end.

There is a specific name for the gap between what you know and what you can use under real pressure. And understanding exactly why that gap exists — and why it quietly grows the more you study — is where everything changes.

Chapter 04

Stop Waiting for "Someday" — Start Building Usable Japanese Today

Take a moment and ask yourself honestly:

Why are you learning Japanese? What is your goal? And does what you're doing right now connect directly to that goal?

Studying 10 or 20 new words every day does build knowledge. But if you haven't yet reached the starting line — the point where you actually use that knowledge — that "someday" may never come.

Too Many People Quit Before They Reach the Starting Line

Far too many people give up before they ever get to use what they've worked so hard to learn. Motivation runs out, they convince themselves it's impossible, and all of their effort goes unrewarded. I've seen it happen so many times — and I don't want that to happen to you.

Start Practicing Communication Now — Regardless of Your Current Level

If your goal is to speak Japanese, to have real conversations with Japanese people — then start practicing that communication right now, regardless of where your Japanese level currently is.

The reason it feels impossible is simple: you're trying to speak perfectly. But there's a specific approach to using Japanese before you're perfect — one that actually accelerates your learning rather than exposing your gaps. It's not about performing. It's about practicing in a way that shows you exactly what to study next.

Every day spent building knowledge without building usability is a day the gap widens.

The question is: what does it actually look like to close it?
